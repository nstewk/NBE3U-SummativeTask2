The Sixties
Scoop

Between the 1950s and the 1980s, an estimated 20,000 Indigenous children were taken from their families and placed in non-Indigenous homes.

Scroll to read

"It was a deliberate act of assimilation."

The "Sixties Scoop" refers to a series of policies enacted by provincial child welfare authorities across Canada starting in the mid-1950s. The term, coined by researcher Patrick Johnston in his 1983 report Native Children and the Child Welfare System, captures the large scale apprehension of Indigenous children from their homes, families, and communities.

Without the consent of their parents' or bands', these children were placed in the child welfare system and primarily adopted out to non-Indigenous, middle-class families. Placements were not limited to Canada, many children were sent to the United States, the United Kingdom, Germany, India, the Netherlands, Botwasana, and several other countries.

The Policy

The Ignorant Removal

Following changes to the Indian Act in 1951, provincial governments were given jurisdiction over Indigenous child welfare. Without any additional funding, the provinces had to rely on social workers that had no understanding of Indigenous culture, poverty, or community child-rearing practices.

What they interpreted as "neglect" was often the direct result of systemic poverty imposed by the government, or communal parenting structures that differed from Euro-Canadian nuclear family models. Children were seized without warning, often directly from hospitals or schools.

"And what she tells me is that it was a very confusing day because she was told by Alberta Social Services workers to get “All your little brothers and sisters and get them rounded up, and we’re going to go for drive.”"

— Adam North Peigan

Severing the Roots

The impact of the Sixties Scoop on Indigenous children and communities was devastating and long-lasting. By removing children from their cultural environment, the state effectively severed their connection to their heritage, language, and extended families.

Survivors face long-term impacts from loss of culture and hertiage to increased rates of mental health issues like anxiety and PTSD. Many survivors also report suffering sexual, physical, and emotional abuse in their adoptive homes and later in life struggled with addiction and homelessness. The trauma is intergenerational, with many survivors struggling to reconnect with their birth families and communities later in life.

Reclaiming Identity

The struggle for recognition, apology, and compensation has been led by the survivors themselves.

Class Action Lawsuits

In the 2000s, survivors began filing class-action lawsuits against provincial and federal governments. In 2017, the Canadian government announced an $800 million settlement for Sixties Scoop survivors, recognizing the harm caused by the loss of cultural identity.

Official Apologies

Several provincial governments have issued formal apologies. In 2015, Manitoba became the first province to apologize, acknowledging that the practice "robbed children of their culture." However, advocates emphasize that apologies must be paired with fundamental changes to the current child welfare system.

The Millennium Scoop

The tragedy is not entirely in the past. Today, Indigenous children remain vastly overrepresented in the child welfare system, a phenomenon often referred to as the "Millennium Scoop." Indigenous communities continue to fight for jurisdiction over their own child and family services.
